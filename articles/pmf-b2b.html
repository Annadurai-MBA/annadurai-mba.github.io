The Robot on the Factory Floor Does Not Have PMF Yet

Using humanoid robots to read B2B industrial product-market fit signals

Raja Annadurai May 2026 7 min read

Two humanoid robots walked into a factory. One of them has observable leading signals of product-market fit. The other does not yet have externally verifiable signals. Both are impressive pieces of engineering. That is not the same thing.

Figure AI's Figure 02 has been running on the BMW Spartanburg production line since 2024, handling real parts in a real production environment with real consequences for getting it wrong. Tesla Optimus has been in Tesla's own factories longer, has received more press coverage, and has been announced with more ambition. Elon Musk has committed to producing one million units annually by late 2026.

And yet, applying a proper industrial PMF framework to both of them produces a picture quite different from what the headlines suggest.

This is not an article about robots. It is an article about how to read PMF signals in industrial B2B markets, using a story that half the people in your LinkedIn feed are already arguing about.

What the news actually says

Figure AI signed a commercial deployment agreement with BMW Manufacturing in 2024. By early 2026, Figure 02 and its successor Figure 03 were handling parts in BMW's South Carolina facility. The company has announced expansion targets across logistics and manufacturing customers through 2026 and 2027. Most importantly, BMW is a paying external customer, not Figure's own factory.

Tesla Optimus tells a different story. Over 1,000 Gen 3 units are deployed in Tesla's Fremont and Giga Texas facilities. Tesla has ended production of the Model S and Model X to convert the Fremont line to Optimus manufacturing. Musk has publicly committed to external customer availability in 2027. On the Q4 2025 earnings call, he described current units as being "primarily for learning and data collection." It is worth noting that Tesla is currently advertising around 200 human roles specifically to work on Optimus, a quiet signal that even the leading internal deployment still requires significant human coordination around it.

The point is not that Tesla Optimus is a bad product or that Figure AI has won the category. The point is that impressive technology and genuine industrial PMF are not the same thing, and the signals that distinguish them are readable if you know what to look for.

The four signals that actually matter

Industrial PMF does not announce itself with a retention curve or a viral coefficient. It shows up in four specific ways, and they are not equal in what they tell you or when they appear. Two are leading signals that appear before revenue confirms anything. Two are lagging signals that confirm what was already true. Reading them in the wrong sequence is how capable products get abandoned too early and weak products get scaled too fast.

Applying this to the robot story: Figure AI has two observable leading signals. Tesla Optimus has none that are externally verifiable, because the only customer is Tesla itself. That is not a criticism of the technology. It is an observation about where each company sits on the PMF journey in May 2026.

Impressive demos create awareness. Reference customers create PMF. The two are easy to confuse from a distance and very hard to confuse up close.

PMF is competitive, not just customer-driven

There is a dimension the four signals alone do not capture: what you are displacing. In industrial markets, PMF almost always means winning against an incumbent under real operational constraints and qualification requirements. For humanoid robots, the incumbent is the existing combination of fixed automation, collaborative robots, and human labour. Figure's BMW deployment tests this directly: BMW already operates sophisticated automation, and adding a humanoid robot to that working environment signals genuine displacement testing.

Tesla testing Optimus in its own factories is a different kind of experiment. There is no external procurement committee to satisfy, no incumbent supplier relationship at commercial risk, and no switching cost to justify to a third party. Tesla may well be achieving internal displacement of labour or fixed automation, and the operational learning is genuine. But internal deployment removes the commercial constraints, specifically procurement risk, qualification friction, and switching cost, that make external displacement meaningful as a PMF signal. A product that works in its maker's own factory has not yet proven it can win under real buyer evaluation conditions.

The economic buyer gap

Both companies face the same challenge affecting every industrial product with a long qualification cycle: the technical evaluator and the budget holder are not the same person. Engineering validates technical fit, procurement assesses total cost of ownership, and finance weighs capital allocation risk. All three need to align before commercial deployment scales.

The economic fit test: A product that engineering approves but procurement cannot justify through the CAPEX/OPEX model does not have complete PMF. It has technical fit without economic fit. For humanoid robots, this test has not yet been passed at scale by anyone in the category. The TCO data is still being gathered.

The numbers make this concrete. At current prices around $100,000 per unit, payback for a Western manufacturer runs to a decade or more. Whether the $30,000 price point Tesla has targeted materialises, and on what timeline, is precisely the variable procurement teams are watching before making capital commitments. The economic fit test is not static. It is a moving target tied to a cost trajectory that nobody can yet verify at scale.

The data integration question that will decide the category

Specification lock-in has historically been the durable moat of industrial suppliers. Once a component is written into an engineering specification, switching requires re-qualification, re-testing, and re-approval. Established suppliers with deep specification relationships are hard to displace even when a technically superior alternative exists. That moat is becoming more conditional.

Industrial customers increasingly require equipment to integrate with their digital infrastructure: manufacturing execution systems, digital twins, predictive maintenance platforms. A humanoid robot that performs mechanically but cannot send operational data in the format a plant already uses creates integration debt. A competitor with equivalent mechanical performance and cleaner data integration may win the next specification cycle even from a weaker brand position. The PMF race is not only about what the robot can physically do.

Three signals worth watching

Three observable patterns are worth following. None of these are predictions. They are simply signals that would, if they appeared, indicate genuine industrial PMF developing rather than impressive demonstrations continuing.

One signal to observe: whether external customers begin publishing operational data. Uptime percentage, cycle time versus human baseline, error rate. It will be interesting to see how this unfolds. Companies with real deployment confidence will eventually have commercial incentive to share this data, because it becomes their strongest argument in the next sales cycle. The absence of published operational data is itself informative.

A second pattern worth watching: second-site expansions from the same customer. A single facility deployment is a pilot. Multiple facilities at the same customer suggests a specification decision has been made. From a commercial standpoint, that progression may reveal more about actual PMF than any first-deployment announcement.

A third area to observe: how procurement teams begin framing their requirements. If RFPs from automotive or logistics companies start specifying humanoid robot capabilities by performance tier, this may signal that the category is shifting from exploration to active specification. That would be a structural development at the market level, not just the customer level, and worth watching closely from a business model perspective.

None of these have happened at scale yet. That is what makes this an interesting commercial pattern to follow and a useful lens for thinking about PMF in industrial B2B more broadly. The technology exists. The pilots are running. The PMF is still being earned, one reference customer at a time.
